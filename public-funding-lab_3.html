Why this existed

Rebuilding years, on purpose.

The Lab began as a Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund program, made possible by the Marin Community Foundation. Then that funding got frozen by the federal administration.

The League and Marin's team treated it like the off season. A team does not stop training because the game got harder. It gets everyone on the same page, rebuilds fundamentals, and comes back stronger.

So the Lab shifted to basics: eight months on how public funding works, start to finish. Some people needed every session. Others needed one. Either way, public funding stopped being intimidating and became a tool people could reach for.

Attendees spanned every seat in the building: CEOs, analysts, marketing leads, finance staff. Some stayed for the full arc. Some came for a single month that mattered to them.

SEP 16
Why Public Funding, Why Now? McKinsey & Company
Orientation +

Community foundations remain trusted, place based partners for moving public dollars to the ground, even as the gap between administrations widens. The session's throughline: learn the language this moment rewards, and use it without losing your mission.

  • Language has shifted. What used to be called climate work is now framed as disaster preparedness. What used to be called equity and DEI is now framed as economic mobility.
  • Understand three beliefs driving federal decisions right now: skepticism of "the way it's always been done," a push to shrink federal agencies to only what's statutorily required, and pressure to show taxpayers a direct return.
  • Public private partnerships are especially attractive under this framing, which plays to a community foundation's strengths.
  • Know what your organization does not do. Say it in one sentence, and let that sentence guide which opportunities you chase.

"Economic mobility is everything."

OCT 21
Public Funding 101: A Level Set Karen Aceves, Arken Strategies
Fundamentals +

Not all grant dollars are the same. Every source comes with its own rules and flexibility, what this field calls the colors of money. Getting fluent in that difference is the real starting point.

  • Formula grants are allocated automatically, often based on population data. Competitive grants require an application and a case.
  • Never cold apply. Call the agency contact first. It is the fastest way to learn what a winning application looks like, and to hear about adjacent opportunities nobody posts publicly.
  • Elected officials want wins too. A letter of support from your representative costs them little and helps your application stand out.

NOV 18
Impact Investing: Financing Social Change Antony Bugg-Levine
Capital Stacking +

Foundations are financial entities as much as mission driven ones. Treating that as a fact, not a tension, changes how you organize capital.

  • Build dollars support the organization itself: staffing up, figuring out what a community needs, filling revenue gaps, scaling.
  • Buy dollars support the programs foundations run: after school programs, food banks, wildfire recovery.
  • Government is the best buyer of proven outcomes, but it rarely funds the build side. Match government buy money with donors willing to fund build.
  • Pitch a build funder with a build story and a buy funder with a buy story. Cultivate trust with a funding source long before you need their capital.
  • The number of billionaires grew from roughly 800 to 1,100 in a single year. There is real, uncommitted generosity on the sidelines waiting to be organized.

DEC 16
State Funding Deep Dive Veronica Villalobos Cruz & Martha Aceves Guzman
State Dollars +

California faces a projected budget deficit, and real opportunity remains anyway, especially around climate. Protecting what you already have matters as much as chasing what's new.

  • Prop 4, the Climate Bond, holds a $10 billion pot with grant opportunities tied to it.
  • Cap and Invest moves roughly $1 billion annually, including $85 million earmarked for climate focused technological innovation.
  • Advocacy that works: stick together in coalitions, build relationships with legislative staff early and stay patient, bring data and real community examples, and track budget and legislative calendars so your timing lines up.
  • Engage electeds outside the standard meeting: honor them with an award, invite them to a ribbon cutting, or give them a keynote slot.

JAN 20
Federal Funding Deep Dive Danielle Fulfs, Senator Schiff's Office
Federal Dollars +

A staffer's-eye view of how appropriations work. Danielle walked the room through congressional apportionments, how funding requests get submitted, the budget cycle across bills, and what happens once you're funded.

  • A follow up webinar ran the moment funding requests opened, so members could act while it was fresh.
  • At least one participating foundation has already submitted a request, currently in process.
FEB 17
Making the Pitch Fran Seegull
Narrative +

California foundations hold more national influence than most realize. The work is to decide, deliberately, what to defend and what to push forward, and to claim the space accordingly.

  • Other states already look to California as a direction setter. Nobody else is telling California's story at the national tables that matter, which leaves the story open to claim.
  • Larger foundations should sponsor smaller ones and bring legislators to the table, rather than every foundation showing up alone.
MAR 17
Open Lab Q&A Sarah Moffat, Arken Strategies
Practice +

An open floor, mostly spent on one question: how do you approach a legislator? The answer that stuck: partner with them long before you have an ask, and build the relationship on its own terms first.

What we learned

Five ideas, one system.

Five ideas surfaced again and again across eight sessions. Together they form a system, each one reinforcing the others.

01

Build the repository

A standing, low stakes place to learn: a playbook, short videos, a lesson archive, and a support group that meets monthly or quarterly. Public funding should never be the barrier between a community and its resources.

02

Larger foundations fund smaller ones first

Before spending capacity on national tables, larger California foundations should fund the state foundations that need it. A stronger bench statewide strengthens every individual seat too.

03

One statewide table

A dedicated space for statewide strategy and voice, so that when foundations sit down with legislators, they're working from the same playbook and the same language.

04

Train finance staff before the need arises

Budget and grants training for state and federal dollars should happen well ahead of a live opportunity. Finance staff especially need fluency long before the deadline hits.

05

Own the story

Every foundation needs its own story, and California foundations need a collective one. Storytelling, branding, and talking points secure a seat at the table.

These five ideas function as a system. A repository works only if people are trained to use it. A statewide table works only if the story behind it is clear. Both collapse if smaller foundations get left behind.
